# Blog post template — "Mulling"

A scaffold Emma can fill in for each new post. Copy this into a new file (e.g. `mulling/my-new-post.md`), paste your text into the sections, and hand it back. Chris (or the site creator) converts to HTML.

Each section has a **guide** (the shape of the content) and an **example** drawn from "Pain isn't damage."

---

## Frontmatter (metadata)

```yaml
Title: The short, confident title. A statement preferred over a question.
Kicker: Pain Science  (the category pill — e.g. Pain Science, Recovery, Running, Mindset)
Read time: 12 min
Date: 21 April 2026
Featured image: path to a 1600×900 photograph
Tags: Pain Science, Recovery, Evidence  (three max)
```

## 1. Hook paragraph

**Guide.** Open with a claim, a story, or a confession. Short. First line visible in social previews. Establishes the tension the rest of the piece resolves.

**Example.** *"For twenty years I read papers. Thousands of them. When I finally moved out of academic research and into clinical work, the thing that struck me is how differently most of my clients think about pain compared to what the research actually shows."*

---

## 2. Why it matters (the setup)

**Guide.** Two or three paragraphs on why the reader should care. Stakes. Who this applies to. What happens if they carry on thinking the old way.

---

## 3. What the evidence actually says (or: what I've seen in clinic)

**Guide.** The substance. Weave in a pull-quote (Source Serif italic on the site — teal left rule). One or two specifics from research or from your own practice. Citations as `(Author, Year)` inline — don't interrupt flow.

**Pull-quote example.** *"Pain is the brain's best guess at whether there's a threat worth acting on — not a readout from the tissue."*

---

## 4. How this changes treatment (or: how I work with it)

**Guide.** Translate the evidence into what it means in clinic. What changes. What you do about it. A bulleted list here works well — four items is a good ceiling.

---

## 5. What to do with this

**Guide.** The take-away for the reader. Two concrete actions, ideally. One of them being "come and see me" when it genuinely applies.

---

## 6. Close

**Guide.** One or two lines that bring it back to the hook. No summary. No "in conclusion." The reader closes the tab feeling the thing.

**Example.** *"Pain is useful information. It's just not the information most people think it is."*

---

## 7. Closing CTA card

The site adds a teal CTA card automatically at the end of each post. It reads:

> **Think this might apply to you?**
> A first session is 60 minutes — we'll work out what's actually going on, and what's worth doing about it.
> → Text Emma to Book

Emma can override the copy on this card per-post if it helps.

---

## 8. Audio version (ElevenLabs)

**Guide.** Every post gets a ~3-minute audio version at the top. Generate a shortened read-aloud script (roughly 450–500 words) from the full post — think "the bits you'd read aloud to a friend in the car." Feed into ElevenLabs (Emma's cloned voice preferred; *Charlotte* or *Jessica* as a stock fallback). Export at 128 kbps, save as `audio/<post-slug>.mp3` in the site folder, and the player on the post picks it up automatically. See [audio/README.md](audio/README.md) for the full flow.

---

## Rules of thumb

- **Length.** 800–1,600 words for the written post. ~450 words for the audio read-aloud version.
- **Paragraphs.** Two to four sentences each. Short paragraphs read well on phones.
- **Voice.** Warm, curious, evidence-led. Writing like Emma talks. Not clinical-cold. Not wellness-fluffy.
- **Certainty.** Prefer nuance to certainty. *"Usually helps"* not *"will fix"*. *"Evidence suggests"* not *"research proves"*.
- **Jargon.** Use the technical term, then explain it in one sentence. Readers are smart; don't patronise, don't gate-keep.
- **Pictures.** Up to three: one featured image at the top (1600×900), optional one or two inline (1200×800).
- **Tags.** No more than three per post. Used for topic clusters across the blog.
